The Scenario

You wake up one morning to an email or text message you weren't expecting. The subject line reads something like "CONGRATULATIONS — YOU ARE A WINNER!" or "Global Lotto Sweepstakes — Prize Claim Notice." You don't remember buying a ticket or entering any contest, but the message claims you have won a life-changing sum of money.

The message tells you to visit a website to confirm your win. You click the link and land on a page that looks professional — it shows the prize amount, a few "real winners" with names and amounts, and a big button inviting you to claim your prize.

  • They provide a website where you can verify your winning status.
  • They tell you to fill in personal information so they can reach you.
  • They request your full name, email, phone number, and country.
  • Later, they ask for a scan of your ID, your bank details, and more.

After some time, the victim discovers they have paid hundreds or thousands of dollars in "processing fees," "taxes," "insurance bonds," and "clearance certificates" — all for a prize that never arrives. The scammers disappear and the victim is left with empty pockets and compromised personal information.

What Really Happens Behind The Scenes

Scammers obtain your contact information through various channels:

  • Data breaches and dark web databases (Facebook, LinkedIn, etc.)
  • Automated bots scraping public social media profiles
  • Purchased marketing lists from data brokers
  • "Sucker lists" sold between scam rings (if you've responded to a scam before)
  • Random number generation via auto-dialers

Once they have your email or phone number, you are added to a mass-mailing list alongside thousands or millions of other targets. The same message is sent to everyone, with only minor variations.

GLOBAL LOTTO SWEEPSTAKES — YOU ARE A WINNER!

Prize: $1,000,000.00 USD (ONE MILLION UNITED STATES DOLLARS)

Reference: GL-8842-NA

Instructions: - Visit our verification portal to confirm your winning status - Submit your personal details for claims processing - A claims agent will contact you within 24–48 hours

Visit: https://global-lotto.42web.io/index.html
(This is a sample of how the site looks)

Scammers often use free hosting platforms (like 42web.io, InfinityFree, etc.) to build these fake lottery sites. The sites look convincing but cost nothing to set up and nothing to abandon once discovered.

The Website Visit

The victim clicks the link and lands on a page that looks like a real sweepstakes portal. Scrolling social proof shows "Ava from Sweden won $16,961 🎉" and "Joseph from Switzerland cashed out $9,802 🎉." A big "Accept Offer" button glows in the center of the screen.

The page explains how it works:

  • Click the Accept Offer button
  • Fill in the necessary information
  • You'll be reached out to via the email address provided

At this stage, nothing is being asked except name, email, phone, and country. No money. No payment. This builds trust.

What Just Happened

The victim has now confirmed three things to the scammer:

1. Their email/phone is active and monitored.
2. They are interested and believe the offer is real.
3. They are willing to share personal information.

At this point, the victim's contact details are flagged as a "hot lead" and may be sold to other scam rings at a premium.

The Response — Claims Agent Contact

Within 24–48 hours, the victim receives an email or phone call from a person introducing themselves as the "Claims Agent" or "Foreign Payment Officer." This is the first direct human contact.

From: "Dr. Michael Harrison" <claims.office@protonmail.com>

Subject: RE: Global Lotto Claim — Reference: GL-8842-NA

Dear [Victim's Name],

We are in receipt of your claim application for the Global Lotto Sweepstakes prize of $1,000,000.00. Congratulations again!

Your claim has been assigned to my office for processing. I am Dr. Michael Harrison, Senior Claims Officer.

Before your winnings can be released, we need to verify your identity. Please reply with the following:

1. Full Legal Name (as on government ID)
2. Date of Birth
3. Present Home Address
4. Occupation
5. A scan or photo of your valid government ID (Passport, Driver's License, or National ID)

IMPORTANT: Due to recent cases of double-claiming, you are advised to keep this winning confidential until your funds have been fully transferred to you. Do not discuss this with any family members or friends.

Yours sincerely,
Dr. Michael Harrison
Senior Claims Officer — Foreign Payment Desk
Tel: +44 7700 900482
⚠ Danger Zone — Isolation Tactic

The instruction to "keep this confidential" is one of the most dangerous red flags. Scammers know that a friend, family member, or coworker will recognize the scam. By isolating you, they remove your safety net.

The Hidden Stage — Identity Harvesting

Once the victim sends their personal details and a scan of their ID, the scammer now has:

  • Full legal name and date of birth
  • Home address
  • Government ID number and photo
  • Phone number and email

This information can be used for identity theft — opening credit accounts, applying for loans, or committing fraud in the victim's name. Even if the victim stops paying fees, the damage may already be done.

⚠ This Is the Trap

Once you pay the first fee, the scammer knows you are willing to pay. They will never stop asking for more. Each payment is followed by a new "obstacle" — and the amounts escalate.

The Fee Escalation — How It Plays Out

After the victim pays the first fee, the scammer invents a new reason for another payment. This loop continues until the victim runs out of money or finally realizes it is a scam.

Second Demand — After First Fee Is Paid

Subject: Compliance Hold — Anti-Money Laundering Clearance Required

An AML Compliance Clearance Certificate is required before the transfer can proceed.

AML Clearance Fee: $2,500.00

Third Demand — After AML Fee Is Paid

Subject: Urgent — Tax Withholding Requirement

The British Lottery Commission requires a tax withholding on international payouts. A reduced rate has been negotiated.

Subject: Final Release — Courier & Bank Charges

The bank requires the following final charges before the wire can be processed:

- SWIFT Wire Transfer Fee: $850.00
- Currency Conversion Charge: $600.00
- Courier Delivery: $180.00
TOTAL: $1,630.00

This is the LAST fee required.

Insurance Bond Fee: $3,200.00

Round 1 — Processing fees: $1,050
Round 2 — AML clearance: $2,500
Round 3 — Reduced tax: $5,800
Round 4 — SWIFT/Courier: $1,630
Round 5 — Insurance bond: $3,200

Round 7 — Technical processing: $2,100
Round 8 — "Final release" again: $1,500

Total loss: $22,280+

There is no prize. The $1,000,000 does not exist. Every dollar paid is gone forever.

The Disappearing Act

When the victim finally refuses to pay more or demands their money back, the scammer either sends one final dismissive message or simply vanishes. The email address goes dead. The phone number is disconnected. The website is taken down. A new domain goes up the next day under a different name.

Subject: RE: Refund Request

Your claim has been cancelled due to non-compliance with payment requirements. All fees paid are non-refundable. This matter is now closed. Do not contact me again.

Or — more commonly — there is simply silence. No reply ever comes.

How To Protect Yourself

  • You cannot win a contest you never entered. If you didn't buy a ticket or submit an entry, it is 100% a scam.
  • Real prizes are free. No legitimate lottery will ever ask you to pay a fee to receive your winnings.
  • Never pay via Western Union, MoneyGram, gift cards, or cryptocurrency. These methods are untraceable and irreversible.
  • Never send a scan of your ID or passport to an unknown person or website. This enables identity theft.
  • Never follow instructions to "keep your win confidential." This is an isolation tactic designed to prevent you from getting help.
  • Research the domain name. Search the website address plus the word "scam" (e.g., "42web.io scam") to see if others have reported it.
  • Check the sender's email address. If it comes from @gmail.com, @yahoo.com, @protonmail.com, or @zohomail.eu — and claims to be a lottery — it is a scam.
  • Look for grammar and formatting errors. Legitimate organizations proofread their communications. Scam messages often contain awkward phrasing, inconsistent capitalization, and odd spacing.
  • Use spam filters and DNS filtering. Services like Quad9 (9.9.9.9) block known scam websites at the network level.
  • Trust your instinct. If something feels off, it probably is. Stop engaging and talk to someone you trust.
Remember This Rule

If you have to pay money to receive a prize, it is not a prize — it is a scam. Legitimate lotteries deduct taxes and fees from the prize amount internally. They never ask you to wire money upfront.

Final Thoughts

Lottery and sweepstakes scams have been running for decades because they work. They exploit hope, urgency, and the natural excitement of a life-changing windfall. The scammers are organized, patient, and skilled at manipulation.

The best defense is knowledge. If you can recognize the pattern — the unsolicited message, the visit to a free-hosted website, the request for personal information, the isolation tactic, the first fee, and the endless loop of charges — you can stop the scam before it starts.

Share this information with family and friends, especially elderly relatives who may be more vulnerable to these schemes. Awareness saves money, identity, and peace of mind.
